Gesture Detection for Electric Guitar

The most recent project I've been working on is a combined gesture + musical pattern detection for electric guitars. The project is a collaboration with Prof. Marcelo M. Wanderley, and is part of my PhD research at the University of Trento. The project aims to develop a system that can detect the gestures of a guitarist and use that information to control various effects.

I investigated a continuum of gestures ranging from natural gestures for better technique to theatrical gestures for stage presence. I designed a system that can detect a few gestures using IMU sensors. The paper highlighting the development will be presented at the International Symposium for the Internet of Sounds 2025.

Musical Pattern Detection

I have been working on musical pattern detection for several years — it was the primary topic of my PhD. I developed several systems to detect musical patterns from monophonic to polyphonic, from symbolic to audio data. I interviewed many musicians to understand the level of artistic and expressive variation that might be applied when they repeat certain phrases. The dataset thus constructed was an amazing resource for my research.

One of my initial prototypes was selected as one of the three finalists for the MIDI Awards 2023. The panel of judges included key figures such as Roger Lynn, Pedro Eustache, and Nina Richards. The livestream of the event can be found HERE.

I have developed several algorithms for musical pattern detection and tested them on various datasets. The system is designed to be easily deployable and can be used with any instrument.

Continuous Blood Pressure Monitoring for COVID-19 Patients

During the pandemic, I was part of a team that developed an IoT system to continuously monitor the blood pressure of COVID-19 patients remotely — reducing the risk of contamination for overworked medical staff.

Our solution revolved around retrofitting components to existing medical equipment. We used the internal connections of blood oxygen meters and webcams with simple image processing algorithms to obtain measurements. Readings were continuously sent to a server dashboard for doctors and nurses to monitor patients remotely.

Automated Quality Checking of Care Labels

Care labels in clothing carry care instructions in multiple languages across several layers. Their accuracy is critical to garment quality, yet checking them manually is time-consuming and prone to human error — especially across languages and symbols.

I designed a computer vision system to automatically check each care label against a reference. The system reduced quality check time by approximately 99.5% and is easily deployable in any factory environment.

Automated Fabric Quality Checking

In apparel production, raw fabric rolls are typically checked for defects manually — a slow process limited to defects visible to the naked eye. I designed a computer vision system using macro cameras to automatically detect artifacts and defects that human operators might miss.

Smart Prototyping for Garment Prints

One of the coolest projects I've done was an augmented reality table to visualize prints on finished garments. A projector and camera mounted above a table captured the silhouette of garments and projected selected designs directly onto them — letting customers see how a garment would look with different prints before production.

I was the lead designer and developer. The video below shows an initial prototype; the final outcome is under NDA.
